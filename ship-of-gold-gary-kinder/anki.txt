What was the SS Central America and why did it matter?	A side-wheel steamer that sank in 1857 carrying ~21 tons of California gold ($300M+ in today's value). Its loss contributed to the Panic of 1857, one of the worst financial crises in American history. Also the worst peacetime maritime disaster in the U.S. at that time.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1857-narrative concept::ss-central-america type::definition
How did Captain Herndon respond as the SS Central America sank?	He stayed aboard, prioritized women and children for rescue, gave his watch to a passenger for his wife, and went down with the ship. 425 people died. The town of Herndon, Virginia and a Naval Academy monument honor him.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1857-narrative concept::herndon-sacrifice type::application
How did the sinking of one ship trigger a nationwide financial crisis?	The SS Central America carried a significant portion of the gold backing New York banks' notes. When it sank, banks couldn't meet obligations. The Panic of 1857 followed — demonstrating catastrophic fragility from concentrating critical resources on a single vessel.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1857-narrative concept::single-point-of-failure type::application
How did Tommy Thompson prepare for the treasure hunt before spending any of his own money?	He spent four years at Battelle Memorial Institute learning ocean engineering on their budget, then years in libraries studying insurance records, survivor accounts, weather data, and every past salvage failure. He learned the failure modes before committing his own resources.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::apprentice-first type::application
What is Bayesian search theory and how did Thompson use it?	Instead of searching randomly, Thompson assigned probabilities to each location based on historical evidence (survivor accounts, weather, drift patterns). He searched highest-probability areas first and updated as new data came in. The wreck was found exactly where the math predicted.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::bayesian-search type::definition
What was Nemo and why was it revolutionary?	Thompson's custom-built ROV (remotely operated vehicle) that could operate at nearly 8,000 feet. It had dual robotic arms, real-time 3D video, and redundant power systems. By using unmanned robots instead of manned submersibles, Thompson eliminated risk to human life.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::nemo-rov type::definition
Why might an engineer who builds custom tools outperform one waiting for off-the-shelf solutions?	The technology Thompson needed didn't exist. Building it gave him capabilities no competitor had. The constraint (no existing tools for 8,000-foot recovery) became the specification for what to build. Custom tools create competitive moats.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::constraint-as-specification type::application
What happened to Tommy Thompson after the book was published?	He sold $52M in recovered gold, paid his 161 investors nothing, fled when ordered to court in 2012, was captured in 2015, and spent 10+ years in prison for contempt. 500 gold coins remain missing. Released March 2026 at age 73.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::aftermath concept::thompson-downfall type::definition
What is the "Founder Pathology" illustrated by Thompson's story?	The obsession, secrecy, and need for control that enable extraordinary achievement can become pathological when accountability arrives. The same traits that let Thompson pull off the impossible made him incapable of sharing the proceeds fairly.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::aftermath concept::founder-pathology type::definition
Why is studying other people's failures often more valuable than studying their successes?	Failures reveal the specific failure modes and constraints of a domain. Thompson's edge wasn't genius alone — it was knowing exactly why every previous salvage attempt failed, so he could design around those specific failure points.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::failure-study type::application
How much gold did Thompson's team recover from the SS Central America?	Approximately 7,500 gold coins, 532 gold ingots, and 20+ pounds of gold dust and nuggets. An 80-lb gold ingot sold for $8 million — the most valuable piece of currency in the world at that time. Total haul valued at $100-150 million.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::recovery type::definition
What does the SS Central America disaster teach about controllable vs uncontrollable risk?	Engineering quality (ship construction) is controllable risk. Weather at sea is uncontrollable risk. The passengers bailed water for 24 hours knowing it was futile. Human effort has a ceiling set by nature. You can prepare for uncontrollable risk but not eliminate it.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1857-narrative concept::risk-types type::comparison
Why did Thompson choose Bayesian search over systematic grid search?	A systematic search of the entire ocean floor would take decades and cost billions. Bayesian search concentrates effort on the highest-probability areas first, dramatically reducing cost and time. Each piece of evidence narrows the search. Efficiency over completeness.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1980s-narrative concept::bayesian-search type::application
What role did trust play in Thompson's fundraising and ultimate downfall?	161 Ohio investors contributed $12.5M based on personal trust in Thompson. He never repaid them. Trust enabled the project (no institutional funding would have backed a treasure hunt) but also created the conditions for betrayal when no formal accountability existed.	book::ship-of-gold chapter::aftermath concept::trust-betrayal type::application
How did the 1857 passengers attempt to survive the sinking?	They formed bucket brigades to bail water by hand for over 24 hours after pumps failed. When boiler fires were extinguished by rising water, all steam power was lost. Two ships were spotted; over nine shuttle trips in heavy seas, 109 were rescued (mainly women and children).	book::ship-of-gold chapter::1857-narrative concept::survival type::definition
