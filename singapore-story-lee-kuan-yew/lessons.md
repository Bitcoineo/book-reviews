# The Singapore Story: Memoirs of Lee Kuan Yew
**Author:** Lee Kuan Yew | **Year:** 1998 | **Category:** Political Memoir, Nation-Building

## The Book in One Sentence

Singapore's survival was extracted from a sequence of near-extinctions by a man who escaped a Japanese massacre at 19, used the communists to win power then destroyed them, got thrown out of Malaysia against his will, and built a country from nothing because there was no alternative.

## Who Should Read This

Anyone responsible for building something that must outlast them. Anyone governing a team, a company, or an institution where the wrong decision means it ceases to exist. If you've ever had to make a choice between what people wanted to hear and what had to be done, and chose the latter, this book will confirm you were right. If you chose the former, it will show you the cost.

---

## Lesson 1: Politics Chose Me. The Japanese Saw to That.

I was 18 when the British surrendered Singapore. 130,000 troops laid down their arms to 30,000 Japanese. The myth of white superiority ended that afternoon. During the Sook Ching, the Japanese screened Chinese males for execution. I was in that line. Something in the atmosphere told me what was coming. I asked to go home and collect my things. The guard let me through. Many in that line were trucked to Changi Beach and shot. I survived by reading a situation correctly. That is the only reason.

The occupation taught me three things. Power is real. Legitimacy without force is decoration. And no one is coming to save you.

**Example:** I learned Japanese, traded on the black market, worked as a clerk for the Japanese news agency Domei. I watched how they ruled: through fear, through system, through the total absence of sentimentality. When the British returned, they were diminished. Every educated Malayan of my generation understood that self-government was not a question of if, but when. I did not choose to enter politics; it was the Japanese occupation that brought politics to me.

**Mental model:** The Sovereignty Lesson. Without the ability to govern and defend yourself, your rights exist only at someone else's convenience. The British held Singapore for 120 years and lost it in a week. Every structure you depend on that you do not control can be taken from you at the speed of events.

**Test yourself:** You are running an organization that depends entirely on one supplier, one platform, or one government's goodwill. What has the fall of Singapore taught you about that arrangement?

## Lesson 2: Ride the Communist Tiger, Then Shoot It

We needed the communists. They had the mass base: the Chinese-educated students, the trade unions, the hawker associations. Without them, the PAP was a handful of English-educated professionals with no ability to mobilize the street. The communists needed us because we were the legal front. They could not operate openly. So we built the PAP together. I drank warm Anchor beer at midnight meetings in Chinatown shophouses with men who were taking orders from the Malayan Communist Party.

I was never confused about what this was. It was a tactical alliance with people who intended to devour me the moment I became unnecessary. They would have put me in a cell or worse. The question was who would use whom, and who would act first.

**Example:** The communists tried to seize the PAP from within. By 1961, 35 of 51 branch committees had defected to the pro-communist faction. They formed the Barisan Sosialis and expected to destroy us at the polls. We launched Operation Coldstore in February 1963: 113 detained. Then we won the election. People who are squeamish about this should consider the alternative. These were not liberals who disagreed on tax policy. They wanted a Malayan communist state aligned with Peking. I stopped them.

**Mental model:** Alliance as Instrument. Enter every alliance knowing your exit conditions. If you need someone's strength, use it. But never confuse a shared enemy with a shared destination. The moment the alliance has served its purpose, act. Delay is what gets you killed.

**Test yourself:** You are building a venture with a partner whose long-term goals conflict with yours, but whose resources you currently need. How do you structure the arrangement so you can operate independently when the time comes?

## Lesson 3: Build Institutions, Not Monuments to Yourself

Most post-colonial leaders made the same mistake. They replaced the colonial governor's portrait with their own and called it independence. They built governance around one man's charisma. When that man died or went mad, everything collapsed. I watched this happen across Africa and Southeast Asia. I decided it would not happen in Singapore.

From the first day of self-government in 1959, we established systems. The Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau was given teeth: it could investigate any minister, including the Prime Minister. Civil servants were paid competitive salaries because underpaying them is just a deferred bribery scheme. We designed ethnic housing quotas, bilingual education, competitive scholarships. These were structures, not gestures.

**Example:** I prosecuted PAP members who were caught taking money. Not opposition figures. My own people. Because if the anti-corruption body exists only to punish your enemies, it is not an institution. It is a weapon. Weapons get turned on their owners. Institutions outlast everyone.

**Mental model:** Institutional Durability. A system that works without its founder is worth ten times one that depends on a great leader's personal virtue. Design for the scenario where the worst person you can imagine takes over. If the system still functions, you have built well.

**Test yourself:** Remove yourself from your organization for six months. What breaks? Whatever breaks is a personality cult, not a system. How would you redesign it so it doesn't?

## Lesson 4: Engineer Integration. Goodwill Is Not a Policy.

The 1964 race riots killed 36 people and wounded hundreds. This was not a surprise. Put Malays, Chinese, Indians, and Eurasians in a dense urban environment with competing nationalisms, and the surprise is not that riots happened, but that anyone thought they wouldn't.

My conclusion was simple: you do not leave racial harmony to chance, mutual understanding, or community leaders giving speeches about tolerance. You build it into the infrastructure. English as the working language because it belonged to no ethnic group. Four official languages for cultural identity. Ethnic quotas in public housing so no block becomes a ghetto. Guaranteed minority representation in parliament.

**Example:** The choice of English was not popular. The Chinese-educated resented it. The Malay community felt their national language was being sidelined. I did not enjoy explaining this. But an ethnically neutral working language meant no group dominated the others in government, in commerce, in the courts. The alternative was permanent communal competition for linguistic supremacy, and I had seen what that led to in Malaysia and Sri Lanka.

**Mental model:** Designed Coexistence. In any multi-ethnic, multi-lingual organization, integration will not emerge from goodwill. It requires structural enforcement. Pick the mechanisms that create the fewest resentments while making separation physically impossible.

**Test yourself:** Your company has teams from different cultures who cluster by language and background. What structural interventions would you make to force integration without creating backlash?

## Lesson 5: Independence Was a Catastrophe. I Wept on Television. That Should Tell You Something.

I did not want independence. I believed Singapore could not survive alone. Two million people on a dot with no natural resources, no hinterland, no army. Merger with Malaysia was our strategy for survival. I fought for it. I won the referendum. And then the Tunku expelled us.

August 9, 1965. I went on television and broke down. Those tears were not joy. They were anguish at the failure of everything I had worked for. Goh Keng Swee was more clear-headed than I was. He had concluded earlier that a clean break was necessary, that the racial politics in Kuala Lumpur would strangle us. He was right. I was still hoping it could work. Parliament voted 126-0 for our expulsion. No PAP representative was present.

**Example:** Within hours of separation, we had to invent a country. We had no army, no central bank, no foreign ministry. The British military base was our largest employer. The Indonesians were conducting Konfrontasi against us. The water supply came through pipes from Johor, and the Tunku had already threatened to cut it. This was not a nation's birth. It was an emergency.

**Mental model:** Forced Sovereignty. The most consequential events in your life are often the ones imposed on you. You do not get to choose the circumstances. You only get to choose the response. Grief is permissible. Paralysis is not.

**Test yourself:** Your largest client terminates the contract that represents 60% of your revenue. You did not want this. You lobbied against it. It happened anyway. What do you do in the first 72 hours?

## Lesson 6: Water Is Survival. Every Other Policy Bends at the Knees for It.

In 1942, the Japanese blew up the water pipes from Johor. That contributed to the British surrender. In 1965, the Tunku threatened to cut our water supply. I understood then that sovereignty without control of your survival resources is not sovereignty. It is a performance.

I said publicly: "Every other policy has to bend at the knees for our water survival." We launched a 50-year water self-sufficiency program. NEWater, desalination, reservoir expansion. The goal was to reach a point where the threat to cut our water would be meaningless. We are approaching that point.

**Example:** People asked why we spent so aggressively on water infrastructure when there were more visible needs. The answer is that a country that can be brought to its knees by someone closing a valve is not a country. It is a hostage. You do not negotiate from that position. You eliminate the dependency.

**Mental model:** Existential Resource Independence. Identify the one input without which everything stops. Then spend whatever it takes to secure independent supply. This is not a budget line item. It is the precondition for every other budget line item to matter.

**Test yourself:** What is the single resource or dependency that, if cut off tomorrow, would end your operation? What is your plan to eliminate that dependency? If you do not have a plan, start one today.

## Lesson 7: The Generation That Did Not Suffer Will Not Understand Why This Matters

I wrote 700 pages at the age of 75 because I was afraid. Not of enemies. Of my own grandchildren. I was troubled by the apparent over-confidence of a generation that has only known stability, growth, and prosperity. They assume Singapore is permanent. It is not. Nothing is permanent. Singapore exists because specific people made specific decisions under specific pressures, and most of those decisions were unpopular at the time.

Whoever governs Singapore must have that iron in him. Or give it up. This is not a job for someone who wants to be liked. It is a job for someone who can be hated today so that the country exists tomorrow.

**Example:** The book itself is the warning. A man writing his memoirs not out of vanity but out of fear that the next generation will take for granted what nearly didn't happen. Every institution I built, every racial integration policy, every water treatment plant exists because we were desperate. The moment desperation is forgotten, the discipline that comes with it will be forgotten too.

**Mental model:** Generational Entropy. Success breeds comfort. Comfort breeds complacency. Complacency breeds the conditions for the next crisis. The founder's job is not just to build the system but to transmit the memory of what it was like before the system existed. If that memory dies, the system will eventually follow.

**Test yourself:** Your organization has been successful for 15 years. The people now running it never experienced the early years when failure was a real possibility. What specific steps would you take to transmit that survival mentality without resorting to platitudes?

## What to Do With This

1. **Identify your water supply.** What single dependency could bring your entire operation to a halt? A key employee, a vendor, a data center, a regulatory approval. Write it down. Then start building redundancy this quarter. Not next year. Now.

2. **Build the institution, not the hero.** If your organization depends on one person's judgment for its most important decisions, you have a personality cult. Design processes, decision frameworks, and succession plans that work when that person leaves, retires, or gets hit by a bus.

3. **Engineer the integration you want.** Stop hoping diverse teams will collaborate on their own. Design the structures that make collaboration the path of least resistance: shared projects, mixed seating, common metrics, a working language everyone can use.

4. **Write your Sook Ching.** Document the hard early days, the near-failures, the moments when the whole thing almost collapsed. Not as inspiration. As inoculation. The next generation needs to know what it cost, or they will spend the inheritance without understanding that it can run out.
