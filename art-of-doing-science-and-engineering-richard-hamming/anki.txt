Why would two equally talented engineers diverge dramatically after 20 years of working side by side?	Because knowledge compounds like interest. Small consistent differences in effort — reading one extra paper, attending one extra talk — accumulate exponentially. The gap isn't linear, it's geometric.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::1 concept::compound-growth type::application
What is the Hamming Question?	"What are the most important problems in your field, and why aren't you working on them?" Hamming asked this at Bell Labs lunch tables to provoke scientists into examining whether their work actually mattered.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::hamming-question type::definition
Why do smart people systematically avoid working on important problems?	Fear of failure, institutional incentives, comfort with known territory, and the fact that important problems are usually harder with less guaranteed payoff. The reasons feel rational but the result is a career spent on trivia.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::hamming-question type::application
What does "luck favors the prepared mind" mean in Hamming's framework?	Breakthroughs appear random but favor people who have thought deeply about a problem space. Preparation means having the right mental frame to recognize an opportunity when it appears — not just having knowledge.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::prepared-mind type::definition
How did Hamming invent error-correcting codes?	Weekend computer errors went uncorrected because no operators were present. Others saw an annoyance. Hamming asked: "If the machine can detect an error, why can't it locate and correct it?" His prepared mind turned a complaint into a foundational idea.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::12 concept::error-correcting-codes type::application
What does Hamming mean by "turning defects into assets"?	Great scientists change their viewpoint so that a limitation becomes an advantage. The defect itself points toward the breakthrough — you don't overcome it, you reframe it.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::defect-to-asset type::definition
How did the slowness of Hamming's rocket simulation become an advantage?	The long waits between calculations forced him to think deeply about each parameter. Speed would have encouraged mindless iteration. The slowness produced deeper insight into which variables actually mattered.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::18 concept::defect-to-asset type::application
Why does Hamming say people who work with their doors open do more important work?	Closed-door workers may be more productive hour-by-hour, but they lose touch with what problems matter. Open-door workers get interruptions but also get clues about what the world needs. Over 10 years, problem selection dominates productivity.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::open-door type::application
What three communication skills does Hamming say are essential for impact?	Clear writing, good formal presentations, and good informal talks. A brilliant idea nobody knows about has zero impact. In any talk, people remember at most three ideas — structure around this constraint.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::selling-ideas type::definition
What does Hamming mean by "tolerating ambiguity"?	Great scientists believe their theory enough to push forward but doubt it enough to notice when it's wrong. Most people need things clearly true or false. Holding both states — committed but skeptical — is rare and trainable.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::ambiguity-tolerance type::definition
What is the "Nobel Prize effect" Hamming warns about?	After becoming famous, scientists stop planting small "acorns" (modest projects with growth potential) because they feel they can only work on "great" problems. Brattain explicitly said winning wouldn't change him — within weeks it had.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::planting-acorns type::application
What did Hamming do on Friday afternoons?	He dedicated them exclusively to "great thoughts" — strategic reflection on what was important in his field and what wasn't. This 10% time investment let him march in a deliberate direction rather than stagger randomly.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::30 concept::friday-afternoons type::definition
How does Hamming distinguish science from engineering?	"In science, if you know what you are doing, you should not be doing it. In engineering, if you do not know what you are doing, you should not be doing it." In practice, both states coexist — all engineering involves creativity and all science involves practical craft.	book::art-of-doing-science-and-engineering chapter::1 concept::science-vs-engineering type::definition
What does "you get what you measure" mean in Hamming's framework?	The way you choose to measure things controls what happens. Like a scientist studying fish with nets — you only see fish determined by the hole size. Metrics shape behavior, often in unintended ways.
