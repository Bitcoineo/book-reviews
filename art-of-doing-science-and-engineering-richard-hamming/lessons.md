# The Art of Doing Science and Engineering: Learning to Learn
**Author:** Richard W. Hamming | **Year:** 1997 | **Category:** Science, Engineering, Meta-Learning

## The Book in One Sentence

Most scientists and engineers could do first-class work but choose not to, and this book is a record of one man spending thirty years at Bell Labs figuring out why.

## Who Should Read This

Anyone in a technical field who suspects they are wasting their career on safe problems. Engineers and scientists who have talent but no strategy. People who assume the greats were simply born smarter and have never been told that Newton said, "If others would think as hard as I did, they would get similar results." Read it if you are willing to be uncomfortable about how you have been spending your time.

---

## Lesson 1: Knowledge Compounds, and Most People Never Start the Account

Henrik Bode, my boss at Bell Labs, said this to me early on: "Knowledge and productivity are like compound interest." A person who knows a bit more than the next person, who works ten percent harder, who keeps investing in their own learning year after year, does not end up ten percent ahead. Over a career, the difference is a factor of ten or more. The mathematics are straightforward. A six percent advantage, compounded over a forty-year career, is enormous. But people do not think in terms of compounding. They see the small daily difference and dismiss it.

The problem is not that people don't understand exponential growth. The problem is that the payoff is invisible for years, and humans are terrible at valuing invisible things.

**Example:** I watched two researchers at Bell Labs who started at roughly the same level of ability. One spent his evenings reading in adjacent fields, learning new mathematics, picking up techniques from other departments. The other did his job and went home. After ten years, the first man was operating on problems the second could not even formulate. The gap was not talent. It was the accumulated interest on thousands of small investments.

**Mental model:** Compound learning. Every hour you spend learning something adjacent to your main work is not an hour wasted. It is a deposit. The people who look like geniuses at fifty started depositing at twenty-five.

**Test yourself:** Calculate: if you improved your working knowledge by just one percent per week, how much better would you be in five years? Now ask honestly whether you have been making those deposits or coasting on what you already know.

## Lesson 2: Work on Important Problems or Waste Your Life

I used to eat lunch at the chemistry table at Bell Labs. One day I asked the chemists: "What are the important problems in your field?" They could answer that. Then I asked: "What important problems are you working on?" Silence. Then I asked: "If what you are doing is not important, and you don't think it's going to lead to something important, then why are you working on it?" They didn't invite me back to the lunch table. I wasn't welcome for years.

But the question stands. Most scientists spend their entire careers on problems they themselves admit are not important. They work on what's easy, what's funded, what their supervisor suggested, what won't get them in trouble. They optimize for comfort and then wonder why they never did anything worth remembering.

**Example:** Look at the record. The people who did great work were almost always working on what they believed was the most important problem they could attack. Shannon was not tinkering at the margins of communication theory. He went after the whole thing. He had the courage to believe he could create a field, and he did. Most people lack that nerve, and so they putter.

**Mental model:** The Hamming Question. Regularly, maybe once a month, sit down and write out: "What are the important problems in my field? Am I working on one of them? If not, why not?" Do not let yourself off the hook with comfortable answers.

**Test yourself:** Right now, write down the three most important unsolved problems in your area. Be honest about whether your current work has any chance of touching any of them. If the answer is no, you need to explain to yourself why you are spending your life this way.

## Lesson 3: Turn the Crank the Machine Gives You

When I was computing rocket trajectories on early machines, the computations were very slow. I would set up a run and then have to wait. I was furious at first. But because I had nothing to do while the machine churned, I started thinking about the problem differently. I would examine each parameter, ask why it was set that way, wonder whether the formulation was right. The enforced slowness made me think harder than I would have if the answer had come back instantly.

This is a general principle. When something about your situation looks like a defect, there is often a way to turn it into an advantage. The people who do great work develop a habit of asking: what can this constraint give me that ease would not?

**Example:** Damn it, I said one weekend, if the machine can detect an error, why can't it correct the error? Everyone else was calling the operator to fix parity errors by hand. I was annoyed. But the annoyance was a signal. The limitation pointed directly at error-correcting codes, which turned out to be an entire field. The defect became the research.

**Mental model:** Constraint inversion. When you encounter a limitation, before you complain, ask: "What does this force me to do that I otherwise wouldn't? Could that forced behavior actually be the breakthrough?"

**Test yourself:** Think of the biggest constraint in your current work, the thing you complain about most. Spend twenty minutes asking whether that constraint is actually pointing you toward a problem worth solving. Write down what you find.

## Lesson 4: Keep Your Door Open

At Bell Labs I noticed something over the years. The people who kept their office doors closed were often more productive in the short term. They got their papers done, met their deadlines, pushed through calculations without interruption. But after ten years, many of them did not know what problems were worth working on. They had been so busy executing that they stopped noticing the world.

The people who kept their doors open got interrupted. They heard half-finished ideas from the hallway. They got drawn into conversations they did not plan. It was inefficient. But those people knew what the important problems were because information flowed to them constantly. They had a feel for the landscape of problems that the closed-door people simply did not develop.

I shared an office with Claude Shannon for a while. I ate lunch with physicists, mathematicians, chemists. This was not socializing. This was strategic. You must put yourself where the ideas are moving.

**Example:** After Brattain won the Nobel Prize for the transistor, he told me he had stopped planting acorns. He was spending his time on ceremonial duties, lectures, committees. The prize changed his information environment. He was no longer at the crossroads of working problems. He was at the crossroads of prestige. His best work was behind him, and the prize itself was part of the reason.

**Mental model:** Information topology. Your physical and social position determines which problems you even learn about. If you isolate yourself, you will solve yesterday's problems with great efficiency. If you stay connected to the flow of new work, you will know which problems matter tomorrow.

**Test yourself:** Map your current information environment. Who do you talk to regularly? Are they working on important problems? Are you hearing about new developments in adjacent fields? If your environment is closed, open it. Deliberately.

## Lesson 5: Friday Afternoons Are for Great Thoughts

For years I set aside Friday afternoons to think about the big questions. Not my current project. Not the next deadline. The big questions: What will computing look like in ten years? What mathematical tools are we missing? What problems will matter in the next decade that nobody is working on now?

Most of my Friday afternoon ideas came to nothing. That is the point. You are not trying to be efficient. You are trying to be right once about something that matters. One good question that you catch early enough is worth more than ten years of competent puzzle-solving.

People tell me they don't have time for this. They are too busy. With what? With the work they chose without ever stepping back to ask whether it was the right work. They are too busy being productive to be effective.

**Example:** I spent many Friday afternoons thinking about what the relationship between computers and mathematics would become. Most of those sessions produced nothing usable. But the cumulative effect of that repeated asking shaped my ability to see what mattered. The individual sessions were often unproductive. The habit was priceless.

**Mental model:** Strategic allocation. Spend ninety percent of your time executing. Spend ten percent asking whether you should be executing on something else entirely. The ten percent is where the real decisions get made.

**Test yourself:** Block out two hours this week with no agenda other than asking: "What will be important in my field five years from now? What should I be learning now to be ready?" If you can't bring yourself to do this, ask yourself what that says about your priorities.

## Lesson 6: You Must Sell Your Ideas

It is not enough to do great work. You have to sell it. I don't care if you think selling is beneath you. I don't care if you think the work should speak for itself. It won't. Good ideas die in desk drawers every day because the person who had them could not write a clear paper, give a coherent talk, or explain to a manager why it mattered.

You need three forms of selling: clear writing, good formal presentations, and good informal communication. In a talk, the audience will remember at most one or two ideas. If you try to pack in everything you know, they will remember nothing. Pick the one idea that matters, make it land, and sit down.

Shannon was brave enough to think he could create information theory. But Shannon also wrote one of the clearest papers in the history of mathematics. The clarity of the writing was not separate from the greatness of the work. It was part of it.

**Example:** I have seen scientists do brilliant work and then present it so badly that nobody understood what they had done. I have seen lesser work, clearly presented, get adopted and cited widely. The first scientist was "right." The second scientist changed the field. Tell me which one mattered more.

**Mental model:** Communication as amplifier. The impact of your work equals its quality multiplied by how many people understand it. If you cannot communicate, you are dividing your impact by a large number.

**Test yourself:** Take your current project and explain it in three sentences to someone outside your field. If you cannot do this, you do not understand your own work well enough. Rewrite those three sentences until they are clear. This exercise will also clarify your thinking.

## Lesson 7: Tolerate Ambiguity or Produce Nothing New

Great scientists hold two contradictory states at once. They believe their theory strongly enough to push it forward against resistance, to follow it when others think it is wrong, to invest years in it. And simultaneously they doubt it. They are looking for the flaw, the place where reality doesn't fit, the evidence that should exist if the theory is right but somehow doesn't.

Most people can do one or the other. They believe fully and become dogmatic. They doubt fully and become paralyzed. The productive state is both at once, committed enough to act, skeptical enough to notice when you should abandon everything and start over.

Newton was not just smarter than his peers. He thought harder and longer. He stayed with problems in a state of unresolved tension until they cracked. Most people flee from that discomfort. They want the answer too quickly. They settle.

**Example:** I watched people at Bell Labs give up on problems because the ambiguity was uncomfortable. They wanted clean results. They wanted certainty before they had earned it. The ones who made breakthroughs were the ones who could sit with the discomfort of not knowing for months, sometimes years, while continuing to push. That is not a personality trait. It is a skill you can develop.

**Mental model:** Productive tension. Commit to your current approach as if it is right. Simultaneously maintain a running list of everything that doesn't quite fit. When the list gets long enough, you will see the next move. If you commit without doubt, you will miss the signals. If you doubt without commitment, you will never get deep enough to see them.

**Test yourself:** Think of a belief you hold strongly in your work. Now spend ten minutes trying to destroy it. List every piece of evidence that doesn't fit, every assumption that might be wrong. If you cannot generate any counterarguments, you are not thinking hard enough, or you are not being honest.

## What to Do With This

1. **Block Friday afternoons for great thoughts.** Protect two hours each week where your only job is to ask what the important problems are and whether you are anywhere near them. Do this for six months before you judge whether it is working. The payoff is not immediate.

2. **Ask the Hamming Question at your next team lunch.** Ask your colleagues: "What are the important problems in our field? Are we working on any of them?" You may not be invited back. That tells you something about the culture you are in.

3. **Rewrite your current project description in three sentences for a non-specialist.** If you cannot do it, your thinking is muddier than you realize. Keep rewriting until it is clear. Then use those three sentences the next time someone asks what you do.

4. **Audit your information environment.** List the five people you talk to most about work. Ask whether those conversations expose you to new problems or merely reinforce what you already know. If your door is closed, open it. If your lunch table is the same people talking about the same things, find a different table.
