# The Art of Doing Science and Engineering: Learning to Learn
**Author:** Richard W. Hamming | **Year:** 1997 | **Category:** Science, Engineering, Meta-Learning

## The Book in One Sentence

Most scientists and engineers could do first-class work but choose not to, and Hamming spent thirty years at Bell Labs figuring out why, then turned it into a course at the Naval Postgraduate School so you wouldn't have to learn it the hard way.

## Who Should Read This

Anyone in a technical field who suspects they're wasting their career on safe problems. People who assume the greats were born smarter and haven't heard Newton's line: "If others would think as hard as I did, they would get similar results." Engineers who have talent but no strategy for deploying it. Skip it if you're looking for specific technical content. This is a book about how to think about your career while you still have time to change course.

---

## Lesson 1: Knowledge Compounds, and Most People Never Start the Account

Hamming's boss at Bell Labs, Henrik Bode, told him early on: "Knowledge and productivity are like compound interest." Someone who knows a bit more, works ten percent harder, and keeps investing in their own learning year after year doesn't end up ten percent ahead. Over a forty-year career, the gap is a factor of ten or more. The math is simple. A six percent advantage compounded over decades is enormous. But people don't think in terms of compounding. They see the small daily difference and shrug.

Hamming watched this play out repeatedly at Bell Labs. Two researchers starting at roughly the same ability level. One spent evenings reading in adjacent fields, learning new mathematics, picking up techniques from other departments. The other did his job and went home. After ten years the first guy was working on problems the second couldn't even formulate. Not a talent gap. A compound interest gap.

**Example:** Hamming points out that the people who look like geniuses at fifty started depositing at twenty-five. Every hour spent learning something adjacent to your main work isn't wasted. It's a deposit. Most people coast on what they already know and then wonder why their career plateaus.

**Mental model:** Compound learning. Small, consistent investments in knowledge pay off on a curve that looks flat for years and then goes vertical. The trick is that the payoff is invisible during the years when it matters most to keep depositing.

**Test yourself:** If you improved your working knowledge by one percent per week, how much better would you be in five years? Now ask honestly whether you've been making those deposits or coasting.

## Lesson 2: Work on Important Problems or Waste Your Life

This is the story Hamming was most famous for telling. He used to eat lunch at the chemistry table at Bell Labs. One day he asked them: "What are the important problems in your field?" They could answer that. Then: "What important problems are you working on?" Silence. Then: "If what you're doing is not important, and you don't think it's going to lead to something important, then why are you working on it?" They didn't invite him back to lunch for years.

But the question stands. Most scientists spend their whole careers on problems they themselves admit aren't important. They work on what's easy, what's funded, what their supervisor suggested. They optimize for comfort and then wonder why they never did anything worth remembering.

Shannon didn't tinker at the margins of communication theory. He went after the whole thing. He had the nerve to believe he could create an entire field, and he did. Most people lack that nerve.

**Example:** Hamming confronting the chemists. He knew it would make him unpopular. He did it anyway because the question was more important than the lunch invitations. The discomfort those chemists felt was the sound of an important question landing.

**Mental model:** The Hamming Question. Once a month, write out: "What are the important problems in my field? Am I working on one of them? If not, why not?" Don't let yourself off the hook with comfortable answers.

**Test yourself:** Right now, write down the three most important unsolved problems in your area. Be honest about whether your current work has any chance of touching any of them.

## Lesson 3: Turn Defects into Assets

Hamming was computing rocket trajectories on early machines that were painfully slow. He'd set up a run and wait. At first he was furious. But the enforced waiting made him examine every parameter, question every assumption, wonder whether the formulation was even right. The slowness forced him to think harder than he would have if answers came back instantly. The defect became an advantage.

This led to his biggest insight. He was annoyed that the early machines kept producing errors, and operators had to fix parity errors by hand. "Damn it, if the machine can detect an error, why can't it correct the error?" That annoyance led directly to Hamming codes and the entire field of error-correcting codes. He turned a limitation into a research program.

**Example:** The slow computers and the parity errors. In both cases, what looked like a defect pointed directly at a problem worth solving. Everyone else was complaining. Hamming was asking what the constraint could teach him.

**Mental model:** Constraint inversion. When you hit a limitation, before you complain, ask: "What does this force me to do that I otherwise wouldn't? Could that forced behavior actually be the breakthrough?" Sometimes the obstacle is the way.

**Test yourself:** Think of the biggest constraint in your current work, the thing you complain about most. Spend twenty minutes asking whether that constraint is pointing you toward a problem worth solving. Write down what you find.

## Lesson 4: Keep Your Door Open

At Bell Labs, Hamming noticed a pattern over the years. People who kept their office doors closed were more productive in the short term. They got papers done, met deadlines, pushed through calculations without interruption. But after ten years, many of them didn't know what problems were worth working on. They'd been so busy executing that they stopped noticing the world.

People who kept their doors open got interrupted constantly. They heard half-finished ideas from the hallway. They got pulled into unplanned conversations. Inefficient as hell. But those people knew what the important problems were because information flowed to them constantly.

Hamming shared an office with Claude Shannon for a while. He ate lunch with physicists, mathematicians, chemists. This wasn't socializing. It was strategic positioning.

Then there's the Nobel Prize effect. After Walter Brattain won the Nobel Prize for the transistor, he told Hamming he'd stopped "planting acorns." He was spending his time on ceremonial duties, lectures, committees. The prize changed his information environment. He was no longer at the crossroads of working problems. He was at the crossroads of prestige. His best work was behind him, and the prize itself was part of the reason.

**Example:** Brattain's trajectory after the Nobel. He went from planting acorns to attending ceremonies. His physical and social position shifted from where problems were being solved to where solved problems were being celebrated. The work stopped.

**Mental model:** Information topology. Your physical and social position determines which problems you even learn about. Isolate yourself and you'll solve yesterday's problems with great efficiency. Stay connected and you'll know which problems matter tomorrow.

**Test yourself:** Map your current information environment. Who do you talk to regularly? Are they working on important problems? Are you hearing about new developments in adjacent fields? If your environment is closed, open it.

## Lesson 5: Friday Afternoons Are for Great Thoughts

For years Hamming set aside Friday afternoons to think about the big questions. Not his current project. Not the next deadline. The real questions: What will computing look like in ten years? What mathematical tools are we missing? What problems will matter in the next decade that nobody's working on now?

Most Friday afternoon sessions produced nothing usable. That was the point. You're not trying to be efficient. You're trying to be right once about something that matters. One good question caught early enough is worth more than ten years of competent puzzle-solving.

People told him they didn't have time for this. They were too busy. Busy with what? With work they chose without ever stepping back to ask whether it was the right work. Too busy being productive to be effective.

**Example:** Hamming spent many Friday afternoons thinking about what the relationship between computers and mathematics would become. Most sessions produced nothing. But the cumulative effect of that repeated asking shaped his ability to see what mattered. The individual sessions were often unproductive. The habit was priceless.

**Mental model:** Strategic allocation. Spend ninety percent of your time executing. Spend ten percent asking whether you should be executing on something else entirely. The ten percent is where the real decisions get made.

**Test yourself:** Block out two hours this week with no agenda other than: "What will be important in my field five years from now? What should I be learning now to be ready?" If you can't bring yourself to do this, ask yourself what that says about your priorities.

## Lesson 6: You Have to Sell Your Work

Hamming was blunt about this. Good ideas die in desk drawers every day because the person who had them couldn't write a clear paper, give a coherent talk, or explain to a manager why it mattered. Thinking the work should "speak for itself" is a career-killing fantasy.

You need three forms of selling: clear writing, good formal presentations, and good informal communication. In a talk, the audience will remember at most one or two ideas. Three at the absolute outside. If you try to pack in everything you know, they'll remember nothing. Pick the one idea that matters, make it land, and sit down.

Shannon didn't just create information theory. He wrote one of the clearest papers in the history of mathematics. The clarity of the writing wasn't separate from the greatness of the work. It was part of it.

**Example:** Hamming saw scientists do brilliant work and present it so badly that nobody understood what they'd done. He saw lesser work, clearly presented, get adopted and cited widely. The first scientist was "right." The second scientist changed the field. He'd ask: tell me which one mattered more.

**Mental model:** Communication as amplifier. The impact of your work equals its quality multiplied by how many people understand it. If you can't communicate, you're dividing your impact by a large number.

**Test yourself:** Take your current project and explain it in three sentences to someone outside your field. If you can't do this, you don't understand your own work well enough. Rewrite until those sentences are clear.

## Lesson 7: Tolerate Ambiguity or Produce Nothing New

Great scientists hold two contradictory states at once. They believe their theory strongly enough to push it forward against resistance, to follow it when others think it's wrong, to invest years in it. Simultaneously they doubt it. They're looking for the flaw, the place where reality doesn't fit, the evidence that should exist but doesn't.

Most people can do one or the other. They believe fully and become dogmatic. They doubt fully and become paralyzed. The productive state is both at once: committed enough to act, skeptical enough to notice when you should abandon everything and start over.

Hamming pointed to Newton. Not just smarter than his peers. He thought harder and longer. He stayed with problems in a state of unresolved tension until they cracked. Most people flee from that discomfort. They want the answer too quickly. They settle.

**Example:** Hamming watched people at Bell Labs give up on problems because the ambiguity was uncomfortable. They wanted clean results, certainty before they'd earned it. The ones who made breakthroughs could sit with the discomfort of not knowing for months, sometimes years, while continuing to push. He insisted this wasn't a personality trait. It's a skill you can develop.

**Mental model:** Productive tension. Commit to your current approach as if it's right. Simultaneously keep a running list of everything that doesn't quite fit. When the list gets long enough, you'll see the next move. Without commitment you never get deep enough. Without doubt you miss the signals.

**Test yourself:** Think of a belief you hold strongly in your work. Spend ten minutes trying to destroy it. List every piece of evidence that doesn't fit, every assumption that might be wrong. If you can't generate any counterarguments, you're not thinking hard enough.

## What to Do With This

1. **Block Friday afternoons for great thoughts.** Protect two hours each week where your only job is to ask what the important problems are and whether you're anywhere near them. Do this for six months before you judge whether it's working. The payoff isn't immediate.

2. **Ask the Hamming Question at your next team lunch.** "What are the important problems in our field? Are we working on any of them?" You may not get invited back. That tells you something about the culture you're in.

3. **Rewrite your current project description in three sentences for a non-specialist.** If you can't, your thinking is muddier than you realize. Those three sentences should become your default answer when someone asks what you do.

4. **Audit your information environment.** List the five people you talk to most about work. Are those conversations exposing you to new problems or reinforcing what you already know? If your lunch table is the same people talking about the same things, find a different table.
