# Made in America
**Author:** Sam Walton (with John Huey) | **Year:** 1992 | **Category:** Business Autobiography

## The Book in One Sentence

There is only one boss, the customer, and he can fire everybody in the company from the chairman on down simply by spending his money somewhere else.

## Who Should Read This

If you run a business, manage people, or just want to know how a guy from small-town Arkansas built the biggest retailer on the planet while driving an old pickup truck with dog cages in the back, this book is for you. It's not a management textbook. It's a country boy telling you exactly what worked, what didn't, and why he made Helen dance the hula with him at a shareholder meeting. Skip it if you want theory. This is all dirt-under-the-fingernails practice.

## Lesson 1: Steal Every Good Idea You Can Find

I've said this so many times people are probably sick of hearing it, but it's the truth. Most everything I've done I've copied from somebody else. When Kmart was eating our lunch in the early days, I'd fly my little plane over their parking lots and count the cars. I'd go walk their stores with a tape recorder. I'd see what Sol Price was doing at FedMart out in California and bring it back to Bentonville. The key is you don't just steal the idea. You improve on it. Add a little something. Then share it across all your stores before the other guy even knows what you took.

**Example:** When Sol Price started Price Club, I flew out to San Diego, walked the warehouse, talked to Sol, came home and started Sam's Club. Sol told people later that I was the best student he ever had. I take that as a compliment.

**Mental model:** The copying chain. Every retailer watches every other retailer. The winners are the ones who copy fastest and improve what they copy. Original ideas are overrated. Speed of adoption is what counts.

**Test yourself:** Why would copying competitors openly (rather than trying to invent everything yourself) give you a structural advantage in retail?

## Lesson 2: Your People Know More Than You Do

The best ideas at Wal-Mart have always come from our associates, never from some vice president sitting in an office. A door greeter in a store in Crowley, Louisiana came up with the idea of greeting customers at the door. We tried it in her store, it worked, and within a year every Wal-Mart in America had a greeter. That one hourly associate created something that became part of our identity. And she didn't need a committee meeting to do it. The Saturday morning meeting in Bentonville exists for one reason: so the folks running stores can tell us what's working and what isn't. I'm just there to listen.

**Example:** A store in a college town started stocking moon pies and RC Cola near the checkout because the associates knew their customers. Sales in that category went through the roof. Corporate never would've thought of that.

**Mental model:** Information flows from the floor up, not from the top down. The farther you are from the cash register, the less you know. A good manager's job is to build a system where the people closest to the customer have a voice.

**Test yourself:** Why would a store associate in rural Arkansas spot a sales opportunity faster than a buyer at corporate headquarters?

## Lesson 3: Keep Your Costs Lower Than Everybody Else

Here's something that drove people crazy about me. I refused to buy a fancy car. "What am I supposed to haul my dogs around in, a Rolls Royce?" Our offices in Bentonville looked like a warehouse. We put executives two to an office. When we traveled, we doubled up in hotel rooms and ate at family restaurants. People thought I was just being cheap. They missed the point entirely. Every dollar we waste in overhead is a dollar we can't pass on to our customers in lower prices. And lower prices are the whole ballgame. When I was operating the Ben Franklin store in Newport, I learned that if you buy it low, stack it high, and sell it cheap, the volume will more than make up for the lower margin. That never stopped being true.

**Example:** Wal-Mart's operating expenses ran about 2% lower than the competition for decades. On billions of dollars in sales, 2% is an ocean of money. That 2% is why we could price lower and still make a profit. It came from things like squeezing the advertising budget, buying in bulk, running a tighter distribution system, and yes, from doubling up in hotel rooms.

**Mental model:** The low-cost operator wins. Not because low cost is glamorous, but because low cost means low prices, and low prices bring volume, and volume brings buying power, and buying power means even lower costs. It's a flywheel. Once it starts spinning, it's awfully hard for a competitor to stop it.

**Test yourself:** Why would keeping your corporate overhead ugly and spartan actually give you a competitive advantage over a rival with nicer offices?

## Lesson 4: Swim Upstream

The whole history of Wal-Mart is going against what everybody said you should do. When we started, every retail expert in America said you couldn't build a discount store in a town of less than 50,000 people. We did it in towns of 5,000. They said a company based in Bentonville, Arkansas could never compete with Sears and Kmart, who had their headquarters in the biggest cities. They said we were too small, too rural, too unsophisticated. Well, doggone it, those small towns were exactly where we found our opportunity. Nobody else wanted to go there. No competition. And those small-town folks were just as interested in saving money as people in Dallas or Chicago.

**Example:** Our first real Wal-Mart opened in Rogers, Arkansas in 1962. Population about 6,000. The conventional wisdom said it couldn't work. But we had no rent to speak of, no competition within 30 miles, and people drove in from the whole county. Within a couple of years we were doing a million dollars in sales out of that one little store.

**Mental model:** When the conventional wisdom says something can't be done, ask who is it that decided that, and have they actually tried it? Often the "experts" are just repeating what they heard from other experts. The gap between what the crowd believes and what's actually true is where the money is.

**Test yourself:** Why would a strategy of opening stores in small towns that competitors ignored create a more defensible position than competing head-to-head in big cities?

## Lesson 5: Share the Numbers With Your People

I believe in the old saying that knowledge is power, and I think sharing information with your people gives them power. At Wal-Mart we share everything. Same-store sales. Profit margins. Shrinkage. Freight costs. Every associate in every store can look up how their department is doing, how the store is doing, how the region is doing. Some of my managers hated this. They said competitors would find out our numbers. I said let them. What are they going to do, copy our culture? That's the one thing you can't copy. When a stock clerk in Tulsa knows exactly how his department's margin compares to last year, he cares differently. He starts acting like an owner. And once you get people acting like owners, you've got something no competitor can take from you.

**Example:** When we went public in 1970, we started the profit-sharing plan. An associate who started at minimum wage and stayed 20 years could retire with hundreds of thousands of dollars. A truck driver in our fleet retired with over $700,000. That money came from sharing the profits, and the profits came because those associates worked like partners, not employees. They worked that way because they could see the numbers and knew their effort mattered.

**Mental model:** Transparency creates ownership. People can't act like partners if you treat them like hired hands. Give them the same information you have. Let them see the scoreboard. Then watch what happens.

**Test yourself:** Why would sharing detailed financial data with hourly associates (something most retailers refused to do) lead to lower shrinkage and higher same-store sales?

## Lesson 6: Lose a Battle, Win the War

I got my biggest education from my biggest failure. Helen and I had built our Ben Franklin franchise in Newport, Arkansas into the top-performing store in the whole six-state region. I was doing $250,000 a year in a town that everyone said couldn't support $150,000. And then our landlord, P.K. Holmes, refused to renew our lease because his son wanted to take over the store. I had made the dumbest mistake a businessman can make. I hadn't secured a long-term lease. I had to hand over the store, the fixtures, everything. Helen was devastated. I was sick about it. We loaded up and moved to Bentonville, population 3,000. And you know what? If P.K. Holmes had renewed that lease, there might never have been a Wal-Mart. Getting knocked down in Newport forced us into the smallest town we could find, and that's where we figured out the small-town strategy that built this company. I couldn't appreciate it at the time. I was too busy feeling sorry for myself. But looking back, it was the best thing that ever happened to us.

**Example:** The Newport store was doing $250,000 a year. After losing it, Walton reopened in Bentonville with a store doing $72,000 his first year. A 70% drop. Most people would've considered that a career-ending disaster. Instead he spent the next decade experimenting with discount formats in small towns, and by 1962 he opened the first Wal-Mart.

**Mental model:** Your worst loss teaches you more than your best win. But only if you refuse to quit. The lesson from Newport wasn't "get better leases" (though I certainly did that too). The lesson was that getting pushed into the worst possible situation can force you to find the strategy you never would've found from a comfortable position.

**Test yourself:** Why would losing a successful store and being forced into a much smaller market actually accelerate Walton's long-term success rather than end his career?

## Lesson 7: The Saturday Morning Meeting Is the Whole Company

By Friday night every week, we know exactly how every store did that week. By Saturday morning at 7:30, three hundred managers, buyers, and regional vice presidents are sitting in Bentonville going through it all. What sold. What didn't. What a competitor did in Memphis. What a store manager in Oklahoma tried that worked. It's loud, it's chaotic, people get up and yell, they do cheers, sometimes they bring in a guest like Jack Welch or a football coach, and by noon every person in that room has heard every piece of information that matters. Then they get on planes and go back to their stores. And by Monday morning, whatever we decided on Saturday is already happening in 2,000 stores. Our competitors had the same data we did. They had better computers, fancier offices, smarter MBAs. What they didn't have was a system that turned information into action in 48 hours. Kmart would take weeks. We did it over the weekend.

**Example:** One Saturday, a regional VP reported that a competitor in the Southeast had cut prices on detergent by 10%. By Saturday afternoon, every Wal-Mart in that region had matched the price. The competitor didn't even finish their promotional campaign before we'd neutralized it. That speed came from having the decision-makers in the same room, every single week, without exception.

**Mental model:** Speed of execution beats quality of strategy. A good plan acted on this Saturday is worth more than a perfect plan acted on next month. The Saturday morning meeting isn't a meeting. It's a decision machine that compresses a normal company's monthly cycle into 48 hours.

**Test yourself:** Why would holding a weekly in-person meeting with hundreds of managers (instead of distributing a report they could read on their own) give Wal-Mart a speed advantage over larger competitors?

## What to Do With This

1. **Walk your own stores this week.** Whatever your business is, go stand where the work actually happens. Don't schedule it. Don't announce it. Just show up, ask questions, and write down what you learn. I used to drop in on stores unannounced at 4 a.m. and go through the aisles with a tape recorder. You won't believe what you'll find out.

2. **Find one competitor doing something better than you, and copy it.** Don't be too proud. I never was. Go visit them, take notes, and then figure out how to do it better. This week. Not next quarter.

3. **Share one number with your team that you've been keeping to yourself.** A cost figure, a margin, a customer complaint count. See what happens when people can see the scoreboard. They start playing harder.

4. **Cut one cost that doesn't touch the customer.** A fancy lunch order for a meeting, a software subscription nobody uses, a nicer-than-necessary vendor arrangement. Every dollar you save inside the business is a dollar you can invest in the customer's experience. That's the whole game.

5. **Ask your newest, lowest-ranking team member what one thing they'd change.** Then actually do it. The idea that built our greeter program came from a department-store clerk, not a senior vice president. Your next best idea is probably sitting in someone who's too shy to bring it up.
