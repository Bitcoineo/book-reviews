What is Sam Walton's Rule 10: "Swim Upstream"?	Go the other way. Ignore conventional wisdom. If everybody else is doing it one way, find your niche by going in exactly the opposite direction. Walmart proved this by targeting small rural towns while competitors fought over cities.	book::made-in-america chapter::10-rules concept::swim-upstream type::definition
Why did Walton open Walmart stores in small towns instead of cities?	Kmart, Target, and Woolco all fought over urban/suburban markets. Small towns had zero competition and underserved customers. By the time competitors noticed, Walmart had an unassailable rural network with distribution infrastructure already built.	book::made-in-america chapter::growth concept::small-town-strategy type::application
What was Walton's view on business frugality?	"Every time Walmart spends one dollar foolishly, it comes right out of our customers' pockets." Overhead costs are the enemy. Lower costs → lower prices → more customers → more profit. He drove a 1979 Ford F-150 despite being worth $25 billion.	book::made-in-america chapter::10-rules concept::frugality type::definition
What were the Saturday Morning Meetings and why did Walton consider them essential?	Weekly meetings every Saturday starting in 1962. Walton arrived at 3-4 AM to review numbers. They reviewed performance, shared best practices, and deployed strategies so by Monday, Walmart was already executing while competitors were just starting their week.	book::made-in-america chapter::culture concept::saturday-meetings type::definition
What was Walton's most painful early business lesson?	Losing his Newport, Arkansas store after 5 years of success because he failed to include a lease renewal option. The landlord took the store for his own son. Walton never made the same mistake again. The loss forced him to Bentonville — Walmart's permanent home.	book::made-in-america chapter::early-years concept::newport-lesson type::application
Why did Walton spend more time in Kmart stores than Kmart executives?	He studied competitors obsessively but only looked for what they did BETTER, never focusing on weaknesses. "If you get one good idea, that's one more than you went in with." Competitors are a free R&D lab.	book::made-in-america chapter::competition concept::competitive-learning type::application
What was Walton's profit-sharing plan and why did he call it his best decision?	Launched in 1971, it gave every associate a stake in Walmart's success through stock options and bonuses. Early truck drivers, managers, and secretaries became millionaires. It transformed employees into owners who cared about the business as much as he did.	book::made-in-america chapter::culture concept::profit-sharing type::definition
What role did Helen Walton play in Walmart's strategy?	She provided personal savings as collateral for the first store loan. Set two conditions: no big cities (leading to the small-town strategy) and no outside partners (leading to family-controlled ownership). Also originated the profit-sharing idea.	book::made-in-america chapter::early-years concept::helen-walton type::application
How did Walmart's supply chain become an unassailable competitive advantage?	Regional distribution centers within a day's drive of every store. Back-hauling (trucks pick up goods on return trips). Private satellite network from 1983. Electronic POS data from 1975. Vendor partnerships like P&G/Retail Link. Infrastructure built years before competitors saw the need.	book::made-in-america chapter::operations concept::supply-chain type::definition
What is Rule 4 and why does information sharing create motivation?	"Communicate everything you possibly can to your partners. The more they know, the more they'll understand. The more they understand, the more they'll care. Once they care, there's no stopping them." Transparency creates ownership.	book::made-in-america chapter::10-rules concept::information-sharing type::definition
Why did Walton dance the hula on Wall Street in 1984?	He bet associates Walmart couldn't hit 8% pre-tax profit in 1983. They hit 8.04%. CFO David Glass arranged hula dancers and media. Sam showed up in a grass skirt. Illustrates Rule 6: celebrate successes and don't take yourself too seriously.	book::made-in-america chapter::culture concept::hula-dance type::application
What does "there is only one boss — the customer" mean in practice?	The customer can fire everyone from the chairman down by spending money somewhere else. Every business decision should be measured by whether it serves the customer. Walton spent 80% of his time on store floors talking to customers and associates, not in the office.	book::made-in-america chapter::10-rules concept::customer-obsession type::definition
How did Walton use his airplane as a competitive tool?	He flew over towns to count cars in competitor parking lots, assess traffic flows, evaluate how cities were growing, and scout real estate — all before competitors noticed the opportunities. Aerial reconnaissance translated into better store location decisions.	book::made-in-america chapter::growth concept::aerial-scouting type::application
Why is cost control a more durable advantage than product innovation in retail?	"You can make a lot of different mistakes and still recover if you run an efficient operation. Or you can be brilliant and still go out of business if you're too inefficient." Products can be copied; a lower cost structure cannot be replicated quickly.	book::made-in-america chapter::10-rules concept::cost-advantage type::application
What was Sam Walton's leadership style?	Hands-on servant leadership. 80% of time visiting stores, talking to associates and customers. Hired people smarter than him, pushed decisions to store level, celebrated successes publicly. Competitive, humble, and relentlessly curious until his final days.	book::made-in-america chapter::leadership concept::servant-leadership type::definition
